Vancouver To Banff: 3 Routes For An Epic Road Trip

If you want to see majestic mountains, raging rivers, and forests as far as the eye can see, a Vancouver to Banff road trip is right up your alley. Also, if you want to see wildlife such as elk, big-horn sheep, mountain goats, and bears, the Canadian Rockies is one of the best places in North America for all of this.

There are three routes to drive, and depending on your time, you can complete a circular journey encompassing two routes. Having lived on the West Coast of Canada, the author was used to seeing the coastal mountains every day — impressive at an average of 5,000 feet (1,524 meters), but they pale compared to the Canadian Rockies. The Rockies are double the elevation, with several peaks over 10,000 feet (3,048 meters).

Why Drive Instead Of Fly?

1
Not A Long Drive

Driving non-stop from Vancouver to Banff takes about 9 hours, but a stop to break up the trip is recommended. Golden, BC (a 7-hour drive) is a great option. Other stops include Revelstoke, Kamloops, or Kelowna, depending on your route.

2
The Beauty Is In The Journey

Driving through BC to Alberta, you'll experience the shifting climates: coastal, desert, and alpine. Flying is faster but misses the true West Coast experience. The best time to drive is June through September when the weather is warm.

⚠️ Winter travel is risky due to Coquihalla snowstorms. BC requires winter tires from October 1 to April 30.
3
You Can Create Your Own Schedule

Flying or taking the train means following a set schedule. On a road trip, you have the freedom to leave when ready and stop for photos along the way.

4
Easier To Pack

Road trips give plenty of space for gear. Keep camera gear handy, pack a cooler with snacks, and have hiking boots ready. Pro tip: bring your own pillow — even high-end hotels like the Fairmont may not have great ones.

5
It's Cheaper To Drive

If you live near Vancouver (or Seattle), driving can be cheaper than airfare, depending on group size. Since the pandemic, flights have become expensive. The one-way drive takes roughly one tank of gas in an economy car (SUVs may need more).

6
No Need To Rent A Car

By driving your own vehicle, you skip the additional cost of a rental. While there are shuttle buses and tour companies servicing the Banff area, having your own transportation in Banff and Jasper gives you much more flexibility.

National Park Fees

To enter the Canadian Rockies national parks, visitors must purchase a Discovery Pass, which gives admission to over 80 Canada National Parks. Purchase online, at the park, or at certain MEC stores in Vancouver, North Vancouver, Langley, and Kelowna.

🎉 2026 Canada Strong Pass: Free admission from June 19 – September 7, 2026! No park pass required during this period.

For other dates: a daily pass is CAD $10.50 per person, while an annual Discovery Pass is CAD $72.25. If you plan to visit more than once in a year, the annual pass saves money.

Choosing Your Route

The road trip from Vancouver to Banff primarily follows the Trans-Canada Hwy (BC-1), making navigation easy. Jasper, about three hours north of Banff via the Icefields Parkway, offers stunning scenery with fewer crowds.

One recommended itinerary: drive to Banff via Golden (one night in Golden), then three nights in Banff, one night in Lake Louise, then head to Jasper for a few days. The return trip can follow BC Hwy 5.

Route Highway Drive Time
Vancouver to Banff Trans-Canada Hwy / BC-1 9 hrs 10 min
Vancouver to Banff British Columbia Hwy 3 (Crowsnest) 12 hrs 38 min
Vancouver to Jasper British Columbia Hwy 5 8 hrs 15 min

For a scenic alternative, take BC Hwy 3 (Crowsnest Hwy), which passes Osoyoos, Cranbrook, Radium Hot Springs, and Kootenay National Park before Banff. It's a longer, winding route but worth exploring.

Where To Stop Along The Way

Places like Kamloops, Kelowna, or Revelstoke can cut the daily drive in half and make it easier for families with young children. Regardless of which route you choose, the Othello Tunnels just outside of Hope is a great pit stop — located in Coquihalla Canyon Provincial Park, visitors can walk through old train tunnels in a picturesque canyon.

Kamloops, with 90,000 residents, has all the comforts of a larger city with first-class wineries, golf courses, and numerous lakes, putting you halfway to Banff. If driving the Crowsnest Highway, Grand Forks lies halfway between Vancouver and Banff.

Golden

Golden, BC is a recommended overnight stop rather than making the full 9-hour non-stop journey to Banff. Highlights include:

  • Golden Skybridge Park — includes Canada's highest suspension bridge and other attractions.
  • Boo the Grizzly Bear — housed in a 20-acre natural habitat, allowing visitors to safely observe and photograph him in a natural environment.

Yoho National Park

By traveling through Golden and into Yoho National Park, you get a preview of the beautiful Canadian Rockies. Highlights include:

  • Takakkaw Falls — Canada's second-highest waterfall, with spectacular spray and a picturesque outlook.
  • Natural Bridge — a unique rock formation carved by the Kicking Horse River.
  • Emerald Lake — stunning and well worth a stop. Easy hiking available around the lake.

Best Time To Visit Banff

At 4,537 feet (1,383 m), Banff gets over 10 feet of snow, closing some roads and attractions in winter. Its winters are very long and its summers short.

Winter activities include skiing, snowshoeing, tubing, sleigh rides, and ice skating. The park without summer crowds can be magical in the right conditions.

Summer brings heavy crowds and higher prices. Visiting in early September means fewer tourists while attractions are still open. If visiting in July or August, stay near downtown so you can walk to shops and restaurants without dealing with traffic and parking.

💡 Sweet spots: June, September, or even May and October. You may encounter more rain, but you'll benefit from hotel availability, lower prices, and far fewer tourists.

Cell Phone Coverage

Outside Banff and Lake Louise, there's no cell coverage. Set your GPS destinations while in town — GPS still works without signal, though you can't input new destinations once out of range. Navigating the Icefields Parkway is easy since attractions are simply along the way. If unsure about driving without coverage, visit the Banff Visitor Centre for a free map.

Where To Stay In Banff

The town of Banff is within a National Park, and as a result, there are strict building codes to preserve the environment. Other than the expensive Banff Springs Hotel (Fairmont), you won't find typical chain hotels such as Sheraton, Holiday Inn, or Four Seasons.

Be prepared for high prices — there are no discount hotels in Banff. Weekday rates are cheaper, as prices rise on weekends when locals visit from Calgary.

Where the Authors Stayed

  • Ptarmigan Inn — near downtown, with parking and an amazing buffet breakfast included. Comfortable rooms, though without air conditioning (potential issue in summer).
  • Fairmont Banff Springs — iconic luxury, but summer rates can exceed CAD 1,000/night. Restaurants are pricey.
  • Fairmont Chateau Lake Louise — stunning setting by the lake, but similarly expensive.

For cheaper rates at the Fairmonts, book in winter. The choice between them depends on preferences — lakefront vs. golf course setting.

Looking For Wildlife

One of the best parts of driving to the Canadian Rockies is the ability to see wildlife. Mule deer are common near Golden, elk roam Banff, and the best wildlife viewing is in Jasper — where moose have been spotted swimming across Maligne Lake.

Keep cameras and cell phones handy so you can pull over quickly. When you see cars pulled over on the highway with hazard lights flashing, someone has likely spotted wildlife. Always keep a safe distance — these are wild animals. A telephoto lens lets you get fantastic pictures from a distance without disturbing them.

Animals are not fenced inside national parks, so watch for wildlife along your entire route during the road trip. Most wildlife species are camouflaged and not easy to spot, so stay alert.

How To Save Money On A Road Trip To Banff

The round trip from Vancouver to Banff, Jasper, and back is about 2,500 km (1,550 miles) — covered on just over three tanks of gas in a fuel-efficient mid-size car (more for an SUV or truck).

  • Bring a cooler and snacks. Load up with granola bars, meat sticks, fruit, and water. This reduces the need for frequent stops and gives you your favorite comfort food on the road. If anyone has dietary restrictions, it's especially important since specialty items (like gluten-free snacks) are harder to find in Banff National Park.
  • Reserve accommodations early. Book early to find good rates and ensure availability. Last-minute bookings often mean no vacancies or premium rates.
  • Find hotels where breakfast and parking are included. At expensive hotels like Banff Springs or Chateau Lake Louise, breakfast and parking incur fees — about CAD $35/day for parking and over CAD $100 for breakfast for two. Other hotels offer complimentary breakfast and parking.
  • Buy an annual Discovery Pass if visiting twice. A daily pass is CAD $10.50 per person, but an annual pass is CAD $72.25. Visiting twice in one year makes the annual pass the better deal.
  • Avoid weekends. Traveling Sunday to Thursday can save money on hotel bookings. Most hotels charge a premium for Fridays and Saturdays as locals from Calgary visit on weekends.

Camping?

For families with kids, camping can save money, and the scenic Rocky Mountains make it a popular choice. Renting an RV might seem appealing, but the total costs — rental, site fees, gas, mileage — often aren't cheaper than hotels. Plus, some roads aren't RV-friendly. If you already own a camper or trailer, it can be more cost-effective than hotels during peak season.

Driving With An Electric Vehicle (EV)

"Range anxiety" — the fear of running out of electricity in the middle of nowhere — used to be a real problem for long road trips. That's largely no longer the case:

  • Tesla Supercharger network: ~25,000 charging stations worldwide, with new ones opening weekly.
  • ChargePoint: over 30,000 stations globally.
  • Petro-Canada network: EV charging stations every 250 km (150 miles) or less from Halifax to Victoria.

The main inconvenience is charging time — 30 minutes for Superfast Charging stations or up to 6 hours for slower stations. The technology improves every year. If you run out of power before reaching a charging station, some new businesses offer remote charging services that will come to you and provide enough charge to reach the next town.

Final Thoughts

The drive from Vancouver to Banff and then to Jasper is one of the most scenic and awe-inspiring drives available. The mountains, wildlife, picturesque lakes, and glacial-fed rivers make it an unforgettable experience.

While the summer drive from Vancouver is easy, inexpensive, and the best way to see the Canadian Rocky Mountains, winter driving is not recommended due to road conditions. A return winter trip by air (flying into Calgary) to experience ice skating on Lake Louise, dog sledding in Canmore, horse-drawn sleigh rides, and hiking Johnston Canyon to see its frozen waterfall is an entirely different — and equally spectacular — way to experience the Rockies.
